Image-based Lighting

Goal of Step 15: let the environment light the scene. Until now everything not hit by one of our handful of lights was filled in by a single flat "ambient" constant. That's why Step 12's metals looked dark: a metal has no diffuse, so with only direct lights it reflects nothing but tiny highlights. Image-based lighting (IBL) replaces that constant with the actual sky from Step 14, so surfaces gather light from — and metals mirror — their surroundings.

The catch is cost. Doing it honestly means integrating the whole environment over the hemisphere for every pixel. The art of real-time IBL is pre-computing those integrals into a few small textures at load time, so the per-pixel shader is just two or three texture reads.

1. The gap: what "ambient" was really standing in for

Real surfaces are lit from every direction, not just by named light sources. Sunlight bounces off a wall and fills the shadow under a table; the blue sky illuminates everything facing up; a chrome ball shows you the whole room. All of that is environment light — light arriving from the surroundings rather than from a specific lamp.

Our shader modelled it with one line: ambient = constant · albedo. A flat constant is cheap but wrong in two ways. It has no direction (a point facing the bright sky gets the same fill as one facing dark ground), and it has no view dependence (a mirror and a chalkboard get the same ambient). Metals suffer worst: their diffuse reflectance is zero, so their entire appearance away from a highlight should come from reflecting the environment — which the constant can't provide. Hence: dark metals.

2. The integral we're approximating

Physically, the light leaving a surface toward the eye is the sum, over every incoming direction on the hemisphere, of "light arriving from that direction × how much this material bounces it toward the eye". That "how much it bounces" is the BRDF we built in Step 12 (the Cook–Torrance terms D, G, F). Written out:

outgoing(view) =  ∑  incoming(dir) · BRDF(dir, view) · cos θ     over the hemisphere
                 dir

For a direct light there's exactly one dir (toward the lamp), so the sum collapses to one term — that's the per-light loop we already have. For the environment the incoming light comes from all directions (the cubemap), so this is a real integral. There's no closed-form answer, so we estimate it. The standard move is to split it into two halves that behave very differently — a diffuse part and a specular part — and precompute each into its own texture.

3. Diffuse IBL: the irradiance map

The diffuse (matte) part is easy in one respect: a Lambertian surface scatters light equally in all directions, so its response doesn't depend on the view angle at all. The only thing that matters is the total light landing on the surface, weighted by the cosine law (light arriving edge-on counts less). That total, as a function of the surface normal, is called irradiance.

Because it's a cosine-weighted average over a whole hemisphere, irradiance is extremely blurry — neighbouring normals see almost the same hemisphere, so the result changes slowly across directions. We precompute it into a tiny irradiance cubemap (32² per face is plenty): for each output direction, walk a grid over the hemisphere around it, sample the sky, and accumulate colour · cos θ · sin θ. That's exactly what irradiance_convolution.frag does. At runtime the shader reads one texel of this map by the surface normal:

diffuseIBL = texture(uIrradianceMap, N).rgb · albedo

A ground plane now picks up a warm bounce from below and the object tops go slightly sky-blue — the directionality the flat constant never had.

4. Specular IBL: why we split the sum

The specular (shiny) part is the hard one. A mirror reflects a razor-sharp copy of its surroundings; a rough metal reflects a smeared blob; and the exact shape depends on the view direction, the roughness, and the material's base reflectance F0 — all tangled inside the same integral. Doing that per pixel is hopeless.

The breakthrough (Karis / Epic Games, 2013) is the split-sum approximation: split the specular integral into a product of two pieces that can each be baked ahead of time —

specularIBL  ≈   prefilteredColor(R, roughness)   ×   ( F0 · scale + bias )
                 └─── depends on the ENV ───┘        └── depends on angle+roughness ──┘
                        (a cubemap)                        (a 2D lookup table)

The first factor folds in the environment and the roughness; the second folds in the view angle, roughness, and Fresnel — and, crucially, is independent of the environment entirely. So we bake the first per-scene and the second once for all time.

5. The prefiltered environment map (roughness lives in the mips)

The first factor is a prefiltered cubemap: the sky, pre-blurred by roughness. A smooth surface should reflect the sharp sky; a rough one should reflect a blurred sky. We bake several roughness levels and — elegantly — store them in the cubemap's mip chain: mip 0 is roughness 0 (sharp), and each smaller mip is a rougher, blurrier version (prefilter_env.frag, baked at 128² base with 5 levels). At runtime the reflection is one textureLod along the reflection vector R = reflect(-V, N), with the mip chosen by roughness — and the hardware's trilinear filter blends between the two nearest roughness levels for free:

R           = reflect(-V, N);
prefiltered = textureLod(uPrefilterMap, R, roughness · MAX_REFLECTION_LOD).rgb;

6. The BRDF LUT (bake it once, ever)

The second factor is a 2D BRDF integration lookup table. Its two axes are the view angle (N·V) and roughness; each texel stores a scale and a bias. Because it depends only on the shape of the BRDF — not on any environment — it is the same for every scene, so we bake it a single time in brdf_lut.frag at startup. At runtime the material's specular response is reconstructed with one read and a multiply-add:

brdf        = texture(uBrdfLut, vec2(N·V, roughness)).rg;
specularIBL = prefiltered · (F0 · brdf.x + brdf.y);

If you want a sanity check that this factor really is environment-independent: a perfect mirror viewed head-on integrates to scale = 1, bias = 0 — the reflection passes through unchanged, no matter what's around it. That exact property is asserted in tests/test_ibl.cpp.

7. How the bakes actually integrate: Monte Carlo + importance sampling

The prefilter and LUT integrals are estimated by Monte Carlo: average the integrand over many sample directions. Two tricks make a feasible sample count (we use 1024) converge cleanly:

  • A low-discrepancy sequence (Hammersley). Instead of random samples that clump and leave gaps, the Hammersley sequence spreads points evenly across the unit square. Its second coordinate is the van der Corput radical inverse — reverse the bits of the index about the binary point (1→0.5, 2→0.25, 3→0.75, …), which fills the interval as uniformly as possible.
  • Importance sampling. Rather than sampling directions uniformly and mostly missing the specular lobe, we draw half-vectors from the GGX distribution itself — dense where the material actually reflects, sparse elsewhere. That's importanceSampleGGX.

These live as pure functions in Pbr.hpp (the CPU twin of the shader maths) so their properties can be unit-tested headlessly — the same way the D/G/F terms have had a CPU mirror since Step 12. One subtlety worth calling out: IBL's geometry term uses a different roughness remap (k = roughness²/2) than direct lighting ((roughness+1)²/8); using the wrong one is a classic, silent IBL bug, so the two are separate functions on purpose.

8. Baking by rendering into cube faces

How do you "render into" a cubemap? A cubemap is six 2D faces (six texture layers). To fill a face we point a 90°-field-of-view camera down that face's axis and draw a cube around the origin — the same cube the skybox uses. Because the cube sits at the origin with no rotation, a cube corner's position is also the world direction that texel represents (ibl_cube.vert just forwards it). Six renders, one per face, fill the whole cubemap; the prefilter repeats that for each roughness mip.

In the renderer, createIblResources() builds the three bake pipelines, a trilinear clamp sampler, and the target textures — and bakes the environment-independent BRDF LUT immediately. bakeIbl() then bakes the two cubemaps from whatever sky was loaded; it's called at the end of loadCubemap(), so re-loading a sky automatically re-bakes (GpuRenderer.cpp). It's a one-time cost at load — a couple of milliseconds — not a per-frame one.

The orientation gotcha. The six capture cameras must line up with the convention a samplerCube uses to read directions, or the baked reflections come out mirrored or rotated and the faces seam. This is the same class of bug the Step 9 sampler swap was — invisible to the compiler, visible only in a rendered frame. The fix is the standard cube-capture view/up vectors (note the flipped up-vectors on the horizontal faces) in cubeCaptureViews(); we confirm it with a capture, not by staring at the code.

9. Putting it together in the shader

The whole change to triangle.frag is that the ambient line becomes the sum of the diffuse and specular IBL terms, darkened by the AO map:

vec3 F  = fresnelSchlickRoughness(N·V, F0, roughness);   // roughness-aware Fresnel
vec3 kD = (1 - F) · (1 - metallic);                     // metals have no diffuse

vec3 diffuseIBL  = texture(uIrradianceMap, N).rgb · albedo;
vec3 prefiltered = textureLod(uPrefilterMap, reflect(-V,N), roughness · MAX_REFLECTION_LOD).rgb;
vec2 brdf        = texture(uBrdfLut, vec2(N·V, roughness)).rg;
vec3 specularIBL = prefiltered · (F0 · brdf.x + brdf.y);

ambient = (kD · diffuseIBL + specularIBL) · ao;

A runtime flag (packed into the light uniform's spare ambient.w lane) switches between this and the old flat fill, so you can toggle IBL live and see exactly what it contributes. The three new maps are bound once per frame at fragment sampler slots 5–7, right after the shadow map — shared infrastructure, like the shadow map, not per-material.

10. Try it yourself

  1. Toggle IBL. Run the engine and press 9. The metal sphere on the left snaps between a dark teal ball (off — the old Step 12 look) and one that mirrors the sky (on). That single key is the whole point of the step made visible.
  2. Change roughness. In Engine.cpp bump sphereMat's roughness from 0.08 toward 1.0, rebuild, and watch the crisp sky reflection dissolve into a soft glow — you're climbing the prefilter mip chain.
  3. Swap the sky. Edit gen_skybox.py, uv run tools/gen_skybox.py, rebuild: the IBL re-bakes on load, so the reflections and ambient recolour to match the new environment automatically.
  4. Verify headlessly. KOI_CAPTURE=out.bmp ./build/koi-engine then sips -s format png out.bmp --out out.png — capture once with IBL on and once off to see the before/after.

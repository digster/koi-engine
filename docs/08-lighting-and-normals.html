Lighting & Normals

Goal of Step 7: make surfaces respond to light. Give each vertex a normal (the direction it faces), add a single directional light, and shade every fragment with the classic Phong terms — ambient + diffuse + specular — over the Step 6 texture.

This is the second half of the roadmap's "Textures + Phong lighting," done as its own step. The spinning cubes now brighten and darken as their faces turn toward and away from the sun.

Through Step 6 every fragment was fullbright: a face pointing at you and a face pointing away were drawn at the exact same brightness. That's why the cubes read as 3D only because they spin. Real surfaces look solid because they catch light — bright where they face a light, dark where they don't. Step 7 adds exactly that.

1. Normals: which way a surface faces

To know how much light a surface catches, we need to know which way it faces. That direction is the surface normal: a unit vector perpendicular to the surface. We store one per vertex (a new normal attribute), and the rasterizer interpolates it across each triangle, just like color and UVs.

For a cube we want flat faces, so all four corners of a face share that face's single outward normal (front = −Z, top = +Y, and so on). This is why the cube is 24 vertices: a shared corner couldn't hold three different face normals — the very same reason it couldn't hold three UVs in Step 6. That split already did the work; Step 7 just fills in the normals.

2. Lighting happens in world space

Here's the subtlety that drives the code change. To compare a surface's normal against the direction to the light and the direction to the eye, all three must be in the same coordinate space — and the natural one is world space, where the light and camera actually sit. But our vertex shader only had the combined mvp, which jumps straight to clip space and throws world space away.

So the vertex shader now also gets the model (world) matrix on its own, and outputs the vertex's world position and world normal for the fragment shader to light with:

// triangle.vert
vWorldPos    = vec3(model * vec4(inPosition, 1.0));
vWorldNormal = mat3(model) * inNormal;   // rotate the normal into world space

The vertex uniform therefore grew from one matrix to two — { mvp, model } — and the renderer pushes both (the model is just the node's world matrix from the Step 5 scene graph). One caveat: mat3(model) is the correct normal transform only for uniform scale (all our objects). Non-uniform scale would skew normals and needs the inverse-transpose "normal matrix" — a noted future refinement.

3. Diffuse light (Lambert)

The core term. A surface is brightest when it faces the light head-on and fades to zero as it turns side-on. That falloff is exactly the cosine of the angle between the normal N and the direction to the light L — and for unit vectors, cosine is the dot product:

  diffuse = max( dot(N, L), 0 )

     light
       ↓    N (full)        N (grazing)        N (back)
       ↓    │                 ╱                  │
    ───┴────┴───          ───╱────           ────┴──── (faces away)
       dot = 1            dot ≈ 0.3          dot < 0 → clamped to 0

The max(…, 0) stops surfaces facing away from the light from going negative (which would be "negative light"). A single directional light — a sun infinitely far away — means L is the same everywhere: just the opposite of the direction the light travels.

4. Specular light (Blinn-Phong)

Diffuse alone looks matte. Shiny surfaces also show a highlight — a bright spot where the surface is angled to bounce the light straight at your eye. Phong models this with how closely the surface mirrors the light toward the viewer; the Blinn-Phong shortcut uses the halfway vector H between the light direction and the view direction:

V = normalize(cameraPos - worldPos);   // toward the eye
H = normalize(L + V);                  // halfway between light and eye
specular = pow( max(dot(N, H), 0), shininess );

When N lines up with H, the highlight is at its peak; the shininess power controls how tight it is (higher = smaller, sharper glint). This is the one term that needs the camera position — which is why the renderer now threads it through to the shader.

5. Ambient, and the final mix

With only diffuse + specular, every shadowed face would be pure black — too harsh, because in reality light bounces around and fills shadows. A cheap stand-in is a small constant ambient term added everywhere. The full shading, over the surface's base color (its albedo = texture × vertex tint):

  albedo = texture(uTex, vUV) · vColor

  color = albedo · (ambient + lightColor · diffuse)   // base color, lit
        +          lightColor · specStrength · specular  // highlight on top

Notice the texture didn't go anywhere — lighting modulates it. That's why the cubes still show the checker, now with shaded faces.

6. Where lighting plugs into the GPU

The light is the same for the whole frame, so its parameters live in a fragment uniform buffer. SDL3 puts fragment uniform buffers in descriptor set 3 (recall: vertex UBO set 1, fragment textures set 2). We push it once per frame:

ResourceSetPushed/bound
Vertex UBO {mvp, model}set=1per node (PushGPUVertexUniformData)
Texture + samplerset=2once per frame (BindGPUFragmentSamplers)
Light UBO (dir, color, ambient, eye)set=3once per frame (PushGPUFragmentUniformData)

7. The code

The normal — Vertex.hpp & Primitives.cpp

Vertex gains a normal[3] (appended, so earlier offsets hold); the pipeline gets a 4th vertex attribute. The cube's 24 vertices get their per-face normals; the plane's point +Y.

The shaders — triangle.vert / triangle.frag

The vertex shader takes {mvp, model} and outputs world position + world normal; the fragment shader adds the light UBO at set=3 and computes the ambient/diffuse/specular mix over the textured albedo.

Feeding it — GpuRenderer & Engine

recordNode now pushes both matrices; recordScene pushes the light uniform once (a fixed sun + the camera position) before walking the tree. The Engine passes camera_.position() into renderFrame/ captureFrame for the specular term.

8. Try it yourself

  1. Move the sun. Change the light direction in recordScene and watch which faces light up. Point it sideways to rake light across the cubes.
  2. Tighten the highlight. Raise kShininess in triangle.frag to 128 for a small sharp glint, or drop it to 4 for a broad sheen.
  3. Kill the ambient. Set ambient to 0 and see shadowed faces go pure black — then appreciate why even a little fill matters.
  4. Smooth normals. Give a sphere (if you add one) averaged per-vertex normals instead of flat per-face ones, and watch faceting turn into smooth shading.

9. What's next

➡️ Step 8 and beyond. With geometry, a scene graph, textures, and lighting in place, the foundations are done. Next come bigger pieces: loading real models (glTF) instead of hand-built primitives, shadows (so objects occlude the light), and post-processing. A natural near-term refinement is a material (per-object texture + shininess) so the one global texture/light gives way to per-object surfaces.

PBR Materials

Goal of Step 12: replace the ad-hoc Blinn-Phong shading with a physically-based one — the industry-standard Cook-Torrance metallic-roughness model. Instead of fiddly "shininess" and "specular strength" knobs, every surface is described by two intuitive dials: metallic (is it a metal?) and roughness (how polished is it?).

This changes only what happens inside the Step 11 light loop — no new vertex data, no new textures. But the ideas behind it (the microfacet BRDF, the metallic workflow, energy conservation) are the foundation every modern renderer is built on.

1. Why replace Blinn-Phong?

Blinn-Phong (Step 7) gave us a believable highlight from a shininess exponent and a specStrength fudge factor. It looks fine, but it's made up: the numbers don't correspond to anything physical, a material tuned under one light looks wrong under another, and nothing stops a surface from reflecting more light than hits it (breaking conservation of energy). Artists had to re-tune everything per scene.

Physically-based rendering (PBR) fixes this by modelling how light actually interacts with a surface. Its promise: a material authored once looks correct under any lighting, and its parameters mean something real. The near-universal parameterisation is metallic-roughness, and it needs just two numbers per surface — which is what our Material now stores.

2. The reflectance equation (the shape of the problem)

All the shading we do is an approximation of one integral, the reflectance equation: the light leaving a point toward the eye is the sum, over every incoming light direction, of incoming light × how much this surface bounces light from that direction to the eye. That "how much" function is the BRDF (Bidirectional Reflectance Distribution Function).

We already do the "sum over lights" part — that's the Step 11 loop. PBR just gives us a better, physically-grounded BRDF to evaluate inside it. Our BRDF has two pieces added together: a diffuse term (light that penetrates and scatters back out, soft and view-independent) and a specular term (light that reflects off the surface, the shaped highlight).

3. Microfacets & roughness

The key mental model for the specular term is microfacet theory: zoom in far enough and any surface is a field of tiny perfect mirrors ("microfacets") tilted at different angles. A ray reflects toward your eye only from the facets whose normal happens to point along the halfway vector H (halfway between the light and the view). Roughness is simply how disordered those facet angles are:

  roughness ≈ 0 (smooth)          roughness ≈ 1 (rough)
   ───────────────                 ╱╲╱╲╱‾╲╱╲╱‾╲╱╲
   facets nearly aligned →         facets point everywhere →
   a tight, bright highlight       a broad, dim highlight

A smooth surface has all its facets aligned, so the reflections agree and you get a small, intense highlight. A rough one scatters reflections everywhere, spreading the same energy into a large, faint one. This single dial replaces Blinn-Phong's unintuitive exponent.

4. The Cook-Torrance specular term: D · G · F

The specular BRDF is (D · G · F) / (4 · (N·V) · (N·L)) — three functions multiplied together, each answering one physical question. (Their exact formulas live in the shader and, mirrored for tests, in renderer/Pbr.hpp.)

  • D — Normal Distribution (GGX). Of all the microfacets, what fraction are aligned with H and so reflect the light at the eye? This is the term roughness drives directly: it's a lobe that peaks when the surface faces H and widens as roughness grows. GGX (a.k.a. Trowbridge-Reitz) is the standard choice for its realistic long "tail".
  • G — Geometry (Smith). Those aligned facets aren't all useful: at grazing angles, facets shadow and mask each other, blocking light on the way in and the reflection on the way out. G is a visibility factor in [0,1] that accounts for this, and it shrinks as roughness rises.
  • F — Fresnel (Schlick). Every surface becomes a near-perfect mirror at a glancing angle (look across this page at a shallow angle — it turns shiny). F rises from F0 (the head-on reflectance) toward 1.0 as the view grazes. Schlick's approximation makes this cheap.

The 4 · (N·V) · (N·L) denominator is a normalisation factor that falls out of the microfacet derivation. Together, D shapes the highlight, G dims it at grazing angles, and F tints and strengthens it toward the edges.

5. The metallic workflow & energy conservation

Here's where metallic earns its keep. It selects between two physically distinct behaviours via the base reflectance F0 and the diffuse term:

  • Dielectrics (non-metals: plastic, wood, stone, skin) reflect only a dim, colourless ~4% specularly (F0 = 0.04) and show a diffuse colour underneath — the albedo you'd expect.
  • Metals reflect strongly and tint that reflection with their own colour (F0 = albedo), and have no diffuse at all — a metal's colour comes entirely from its tinted reflections.

So in the shader F0 = mix(0.04, albedo, metallic), and the diffuse term is scaled by (1 - metallic) so metals lose it entirely. Energy conservation adds one more rule: light reflected specularly can't also come back as diffuse, so we weight diffuse by kd = (1 - F). And the Lambertian diffuse is divided by π (the correct normalisation so a surface never re-emits more than it receives). That /π is exactly why our light intensities are larger this step than in the Blinn-Phong era — same brightness, honest math.

  per light in the loop (radiance = color · intensity · attenuation):
     F0   = mix(0.04, albedo, metallic)
     spec = D·G·F / (4·(N·V)·(N·L))
     kd   = (1 - F) · (1 - metallic)        // metals: no diffuse
     Lo  += shadow · (kd · albedo/π + spec) · radiance · (N·L)

6. Why the metals look dark (and what IBL will fix)

Run the engine and the metal sphere is mostly dark except where a light reflects in it. That's correct: a metal has no diffuse, so with only a few direct lights it can only show those lights' sharp reflections — everywhere else it's reflecting the (empty, dark) room. A real metal object looks bright because it reflects a whole environment.

Supplying that environment is image-based lighting (IBL) — pre-filtering an environment map into the ambient diffuse and specular a surface would see from all directions. It's the natural next step, and the reason our ambient term here is deliberately a crude ambient · albedo placeholder. Seeing metals go dark is the lesson: it's what motivates IBL.

7. The code

The material — scene/Material.hpp

shininess/specStrength became metallic + roughness (both 0..1); the albedo texture is unchanged. The renderer pushes them in the same material uniform's two vec4 lanes it already used, so no binding or buffer size changes — recordNode just sends {metallic, roughness}.

The BRDF math — triangle.frag + Pbr.hpp

The shader's per-light body swapped Blinn-Phong for the Cook-Torrance terms above; the vertex shader and the Step 11 light loop/uniform are untouched. The three sub-terms (distributionGGX, geometrySmith, fresnelSchlick) have pure CPU twins in Pbr.hpp, unit-tested in test_pbr.cpp — the shader stays the runtime source of truth, the header lets us pin the math's shape headlessly.

The demo — Engine.cpp

buildScene assigns contrasting materials — a rough dielectric floor, a metal hub and a shiny metal sphere, a glossy dielectric torus — so the metallic/roughness axes are visible side by side. setupLights bumps the light intensities to suit the energy-conserving math.

8. Try it yourself

  1. Slide the roughness. Change a material's roughness in Engine.cpp from 0.1 to 0.9 and rebuild: watch the highlight go from a tight glint to a broad sheen.
  2. Flip metallic. Set the torus metallic = 1.0: its bright diffuse body collapses to dark metal with sharp coloured reflections — the metal/dielectric difference in one edit.
  3. Watch Fresnel. Fly the camera so a surface is nearly edge-on to a light; the rim brightens as F climbs toward 1 — the grazing-angle mirror effect.
  4. Feel the /π. Remove the / PI from the diffuse term and the scene blows out — a quick way to see what energy conservation buys you.

9. What's next

➡️ Texture the material and light the environment. Two directions build straight on this: texture maps — per-pixel metallic/ roughness and normal maps (which need per-vertex tangents) for real surface detail; and IBL, which finally gives ambient and metals a world to reflect. Better shadows (sun cascades, point-light cube maps) also remain open.

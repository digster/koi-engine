Models & Shadows

Goal of Step 9: two things at once. Load real models from files (a sphere from glTF, a torus from OBJ) instead of only hand-built cubes, and add shadow mapping so objects cast shadows on the floor and each other.

This is a bigger step than usual — and the two halves make a deliberate trade: we use LIBRARIES for the model parsing (it's bookkeeping, not graphics) so the from-scratch effort goes into the shadow algorithm.

1. Loading real models — with a library, on purpose

So far every shape was built by hand in code. Real engines load model files exported from tools (Blender, etc.). The two common formats: OBJ (old, plain text) and glTF (modern, JSON + binary, the "JPEG of 3D"). Parsing them is fiddly — separate index lists in OBJ, typed binary accessors in glTF — with little conceptual payoff. Unlike our hand-rolled math (where writing it is the lesson), here we use small single-header libraries: tinyobjloader for OBJ and cgltf for glTF, downloaded at build time like our doctest header. That keeps the budget on the graphics.

Our loadModel reads a file's first mesh — positions, normals, UVs, indices — into our Vertex layout and uploads it via the same createMesh a primitive uses. The engine then gives the model its own Material (we read geometry only, not the file's materials).

2. One index per vertex (and 32-bit indices)

OBJ has a quirk worth understanding: a face references separate indices for position, texture coordinate, and normal (f 5/1/3 …). But the GPU wants one index per fully-combined vertex. So the loader de-duplicates: each unique (position, uv, normal) triple becomes one Vertex, and a hash map turns repeats into a shared index. (glTF already stores combined vertices, so it skips this.)

Hand-built shapes fit in 16-bit indices (≤ 65 535 vertices), but a real model can have far more, so Mesh now records whether its index buffer is 16- or 32-bit, and the renderer binds it accordingly.

3. Why nothing cast a shadow — until now

Lighting (Step 7) darkens a surface that faces away from the light, but it has no idea whether something between the surface and the light is blocking it. That's what a shadow is: occlusion. Computing it needs a global question — "is anything between this point and the light?" — that per-fragment shading can't answer alone.

4. Shadow mapping: render from the light, then compare

The classic trick is shadow mapping, and it's beautifully simple in spirit: render the scene a first time from the light's point of view, keeping only depth. That "shadow map" records, for each direction the light looks, how far away the nearest surface is. Then when shading normally, ask: is this fragment farther from the light than what the shadow map saw in that direction? If yes, something was closer — this fragment is in shadow.

  PASS 1 (from the light)          PASS 2 (from the camera)
  ┌─ light ─┐                      for each fragment:
  │  │ │ │   render depth only       project worldPos into the light's view
  ▼  ▼ ▼ ▼   into the shadow map     d_frag  = its depth from the light
  ═══════════                        d_near  = shadowMap[that direction]
   scene                            in shadow  ⇔  d_frag > d_near (+ bias)

5. Pass 1 — depth from the light's view

Our light is directional (a sun), so its rays are parallel — we view the scene through an orthographic box aligned with the light direction (a new orthographic() joins perspective() in the math). The shadow map is a depth texture created so it can be both rendered into and sampled later. A dedicated depth-only pipeline (a tiny shadow.vert that just positions vertices in the light's clip space, and an empty shadow.frag) renders the scene's depth into it — no color, the GPU writes depth for free.

6. Pass 2 — the comparison (bias & PCF)

In the main fragment shader we transform the fragment's world position by the same light matrix, giving its position in the shadow map and its depth from the light. Compare that depth to the stored one. Two practical wrinkles every shadow map needs:

  • Bias. Floating-point depth and the shadow map's finite resolution make a surface very slightly "deeper" than its own recorded depth, so it shadows itself in an ugly moiré ("shadow acne"). A small depth bias (scaled by how steeply the surface faces the light) nudges the comparison to prevent it.
  • PCF. A single compare gives hard, jagged edges. Averaging a small 2×2 block of shadow-map taps (Percentage-Closer Filtering) softens them.

Shadowed fragments keep their ambient term but lose diffuse + specular, so shadows darken rather than turning pure black.

7. A binding gotcha worth remembering

The main fragment shader now reads two textures — the material's image and the shadow map. When we compiled GLSL → SPIR-V → MSL, spirv-cross ordered the two samplers by usage rather than by their layout(binding = N), silently swapping them on Metal — the surface texture sampled the depth map and vice versa, washing the scene to grayscale. The fix is one flag, --msl-decoration-binding, which makes the Metal resource indices follow the GLSL bindings. A good reminder that the cross-compile step has conventions of its own — and that the headless capture is what catches such bugs, since they're invisible to the compiler.

8. The code

The loader — src/renderer/ModelLoader.cpp

Dispatches by extension: .obj → tinyobjloader (+ dedup), .glb/.gltf → cgltf. The third-party headers are pulled into this one translation unit (compiled with warnings off, since we don't own that code). Mesh gained a 32-bit index path in createMesh.

The shadow pass — GpuRenderer & shadow.vert/.frag

The renderer owns the shadow map, a CLAMP sampler, and the depth-only pipeline; renderShadowPass draws the scene's depth from the light before the main pass. recordScene binds the shadow map (fragment slot 1) and pushes the light matrix into the light uniform; triangle.frag does the lookup, bias, and PCF.

The scene — Engine

buildScene loads sphere.glb and torus.obj, gives them glossy materials, and places them flanking the cubes.

9. Try it yourself

  1. Drop in a model. Export any low-poly .obj or .glb from Blender into assets/, re-run CMake, and load it in buildScene.
  2. Move the sun. Change the light direction in GpuRenderer.cpp and watch the shadows swing — the shadow pass and the shading use the same direction, so they stay in sync.
  3. See the acne. Set the depth bias to 0 and look for the shimmering self-shadowing it was preventing.
  4. Shrink the shadow map. Drop it from 2048² to 256² and watch the shadow edges get blocky — the resolution/quality trade-off, visible.

10. What's next

➡️ Post-processing. The engine now has geometry, a scene graph, textures, materials, lighting, loaded models, and shadows. A natural next step is to render the whole scene into an offscreen target and run a fullscreen pass over it — tone-mapping, bloom, or anti-aliasing — the gateway to a modern post-processing pipeline.

Multiple Lights

Goal of Step 11: stop lighting the whole world with one hardcoded "sun." Give the scene a list of lights of different kinds — a directional sun, several point lights (bulbs), and a spot light (a flashlight) — and let the fragment shader loop over them and add up their contributions.

Along the way we meet the two ideas that make point and spot lights believable: attenuation (light fading with distance) and the spot cone (a soft-edged beam). You can toggle each group of lights at runtime to watch what it adds.

1. Why one light is never enough

Since Step 7 the engine has shaded every surface with a single directional light — a stand-in for the sun. That's a fine start, but look around a real room: a lamp in one corner, sunlight through a window, a screen glowing on a face. Light comes from many sources, each with its own position, colour, and reach. A renderer that can place only one light can never paint that.

The good news is that light is additive: the brightness at a point is simply the sum of what every light delivers there. That single fact is the whole design. We keep our per-light shading math (Blinn-Phong from Step 7) exactly as it was, wrap it in a loop, and add each light's result into a running total. Turning a light off only removes its term; it never disturbs the others.

2. Three kinds of light

Almost every classic real-time light is one of three types, differing only in where the rays come from and how they fade:

  DIRECTIONAL (the sun)      POINT (a bulb)            SPOT (a flashlight)
   ‖ ‖ ‖ ‖ ‖ ‖ ‖              · ·  ·  · ·                    (o)
   ‖ ‖ ‖ ‖ ‖ ‖ ‖             ·   \ | /   ·                  / | \
   ‖ ‖ ‖ ‖ ‖ ‖ ‖            ·  ── (o) ──  ·                /  |  \
   parallel rays,          radiates every way,           a cone of rays,
   no position,            has a position,               position + aim,
   no distance falloff     falls off with distance       falloff + a soft edge
  • Directional — rays are parallel and come from infinitely far away, so the light has a direction but no position, and its strength is the same everywhere. This is our sun.
  • Point — a bulb at a position that radiates equally in all directions. Its brightness falls off with distance (attenuation, §3), so close surfaces are lit strongly and far ones barely at all.
  • Spot — a point light restricted to a cone. It adds a direction (where the beam aims) and two cone angles, fading the light out toward the cone's edge (§4).

Because they share so much, we store all three in one flat Light struct and let a type field decide which fields matter. The shader branches on that type.

3. Attenuation: why a bulb fades with distance

A directional sun is equally bright on the near and far side of a room, because its rays are parallel. A bulb is not: stand twice as far from a lamp and it looks markedly dimmer. Why?

The light a bulb emits spreads over the surface of an ever-growing sphere. The area of that sphere is 4πr² — it grows with the square of the distance — so the light per unit area drops as 1/r². This is the inverse-square law, and it's why our attenuation starts from 1 / (d² + 1) (the +1 keeps it a well-behaved 1.0 at the light's own position instead of dividing by zero):

  brightness
   1 |*                     attenuation(d, range)
     | *
     |  *.
     |    '..                inverse-square core (1/d²) …
     |       '''..
     |            '''''....  … multiplied by a window that
   0 |___________________'''___  forces an exact 0 at `range`
     0        distance d      range

Pure 1/d² never quite reaches zero, though — a distant light would still cost the shader a few instructions for a contribution you'd never see. So we multiply by a range window, clamp(1 − (d/range)⁴, 0, 1)², which smoothly forces the value to exactly 0 at range. Now a light has a finite radius the renderer (and, later, culling) can rely on. This windowed inverse-square is the falloff used in Unreal Engine, among others.

Our engine keeps a CPU copy of this exact formula, koi::attenuation, next to the shader's — the same "pure helper mirrors the shader" trick we used for the tone-map curve — so its shape can be unit-tested without a GPU.

4. The spot cone

A spotlight is a point light with blinders: it only lights a cone around an aim direction. We describe that cone with two half-angles — an inner one (inside it, full brightness) and a wider outer one (past it, nothing) — and fade smoothly between them so the beam has a soft edge rather than a hard-cut circle.

        aim
         │  inner cone → full bright
        /│\
       / │ \  outer cone → fades to 0
      /  │  \
     /   │   \      between the two: a smooth ramp (soft edge)
    ┴────┴────┴

Here's the neat trick: we never compute an angle. The dot product of two unit vectors is the cosine of the angle between them, so we compare cosines directly and skip the expensive acos. We take θ = dot(L, aim) (how aligned the fragment is with the beam) and feed it to smoothstep(outerCos, innerCos, θ). One subtlety trips everyone up once: cosine decreases as the angle grows, so a fragment inside the tight inner cone has the largest cosine — which is why the inner cutoff cosine is the upper edge of the ramp and the outer is the lower.

5. Adding it all up in the shader

With the pieces in hand, the fragment shader (triangle.frag) becomes a loop. Ambient light — the constant fill that stands in for bounced light reaching everywhere — is added once, up front. Then, for each active light, we compute its direction L and attenuation by type, run the same Blinn-Phong diffuse + specular as before, scale by the light's colour × intensity × attenuation, and add it in:

  result = albedo * ambient                    // added ONCE
  for each light i:
      L, atten = by type (dir: no falloff; point: distance; spot: × cone)
      diffuse  = max(dot(N, L), 0)
      specular = pow(max(dot(N, H), 0), shininess)
      radiance = light.color * light.intensity * atten
      shadow   = (i == 0 && directional) ? shadowFactor() : 1     // only the sun
      result  += shadow * radiance * (albedo * diffuse + specStrength * specular)
  outColor = result

Notice the shadow line: only light 0, the directional sun, is multiplied by the shadow term. That's deliberate — see §7.

6. Getting a whole list of lights onto the GPU

One light used to travel to the shader as a handful of vec4s in a uniform buffer. Many lights travel as an array. But a uniform buffer's layout is fixed when the pipeline is built, so the array must be a fixed maximum size — we pick MAX_LIGHTS = 8, declared identically in both Light.hpp and the shader, and send along a lightCount telling the loop how many slots are actually used.

Packing that array correctly means respecting std140, the GPU's uniform memory layout rules. The one that bites beginners: every member is aligned to a 16-byte (vec4) boundary, and a vec3 is padded up to a vec4. So we deliberately pack each light as exactly four vec4s (position+range, direction+type, colour+intensity, the two cone cosines), folding small scalars into the unused .w lanes. Four vec4s = 64 bytes with no hidden padding, so the C++ struct in GpuRenderer and the GLSL struct line up byte-for-byte. Get a vec3 in there by accident and the whole array silently shifts — a classic, maddening bug.

Why a fixed array, and not thousands of lights? Because every fragment loops over every light — cost is pixels × lights. A handful is fine; hundreds are not. Engines that want many lights switch strategy entirely (deferred or clustered/tiled shading, which only test lights against the pixels they could possibly touch). That's a big topic for a later step; a small fixed array is the right amount of machinery for now.

7. Shadows and many lights

In Step 9 we built one shadow map, from the sun's point of view. Each shadow-casting light needs its own shadow map — and a point light, radiating in every direction, needs six (a cube map) or a clever substitute. That's real work, so this step keeps it honest: only the directional sun (light 0) casts a shadow; the point and spot lights add illumination but no shadow.

To keep the sun and its shadow in lockstep, the renderer reads the sun's direction straight from lights[0] and uses it to build both the shadow map and the shadow lookup — so if you re-aim the sun, its shadows follow. Shadow-casting the other lights (cascades for the sun, cube maps for points) is a natural future milestone.

8. The code

The light type — scene/Light.hpp

A header-only, SDL-free Light struct (type, position, direction, colour, intensity, range, cone cosines, an enabled flag) plus the pure helpers attenuation, spotFactor, and activeLightCount that mirror the shader and are unit-tested in test_light.cpp. This is the same shape as Material and PostSettings: plain data the Engine owns and hands to the renderer.

The shader — triangle.frag

The single-light block became the MAX_LIGHTS array + loop shown in §5, with a per-type branch for L/attenuation and the spot cone. The vertex shader is unchanged — it already outputs world position and normal, which is all the lighting loop needs regardless of how many lights there are.

The renderer — GpuRenderer

The light list is threaded through renderFrame / captureFrame → renderSceneAndPost → recordScene as a std::span<const Light> — exactly how cameraPos and PostSettings were threaded in earlier steps. recordScene packs the enabled lights into the std140 array and pushes it once per frame; renderSceneAndPost pulls the sun's direction from lights[0] for the shadow pass.

The scene & controls — Engine

setupLights places the sun (index 0) plus two coloured point lights and an overhead spot; the loop orbits one point light so its colour sweeps the scene. Keys 5/6/7 toggle the point lights / the spot / the sun, by flipping each light's enabled flag — off lights are simply skipped when packing.

9. Try it yourself

  1. Toggle each group. 5 (point lights), 6 (spot), 7 (sun). Turn the sun off and only the coloured lights remain — note they cast no shadow (§7).
  2. Watch the orbit. One point light circles the scene; follow its coloured pool as it slides across the floor and the models — the clearest proof that a light has a position, unlike the sun.
  3. Change the falloff. Shrink a point light's range in Engine.cpp and its pool tightens to a small bright disc; grow it and the light reaches across the whole floor.
  4. Widen the beam. Push the spot's inner/outer angles apart (or together) and watch its edge soften or sharpen. Set them equal for a hard-cut circle.

10. What's next

➡️ Physically-based materials. We now have a proper multi-light forward renderer. The next big idea is PBR — replacing ad-hoc Blinn-Phong with an energy-conserving model driven by roughness and metalness, and richer surfaces via normal maps. Beyond that: more shadow casters (sun cascades, point-light cube maps) and, eventually, the deferred/clustered shading that lets a scene hold hundreds of lights.

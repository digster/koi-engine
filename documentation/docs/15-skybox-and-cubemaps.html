Skybox & Cubemaps

Goal of Step 14: put a world behind the scene — a sky that stretches to the horizon in every direction — instead of a flat background colour. The vehicle is a new kind of texture, the cubemap, sampled by a direction rather than a 2D coordinate.

The sky is the cheap, visible pay-off. The real prize is the plumbing: a cubemap is exactly what the next step, image-based lighting, needs to light surfaces from their surroundings — the fix for the dark metals Step 12 left behind. Build the data structure now with a simple consumer (draw it); reuse it next with the hard one (light with it).

1. Why a flat clear colour isn't enough

Every frame so far has begun by wiping the screen to a single colour, and anything the geometry didn't cover stayed that colour. That reads as "the void", not "outdoors". A real scene sits inside an environment: a sky, a horizon, distant hills — things infinitely far away that you can look toward but never reach. We want the background to depend on which way the camera is looking, and to stay put in the world as the camera moves around.

"A colour per direction you look" is the whole idea, and it's precisely what a cubemap stores.

2. What a cubemap is

An ordinary texture (Step 6) is a flat image you read with a 2D coordinate (u, v). But directions live on a sphere, not a square, and there's no seamless, low-distortion way to wrap one square image around a whole sphere. A cubemap sidesteps that: it's six square textures, one per face of a cube (+X, −X, +Y, −Y, +Z, −Z), and you sample it with a 3D direction vector instead of a 2D coordinate.

              +Y  (up)
               ┌─────┐
   look along  │     │        sample(cubemap, dir):
   a DIRECTION │ +Z  │  ──▶     1. which face does `dir` point at?  (its biggest component)
        dir ──▶│     │          2. where on that face does it pierce?  (the other two, ÷ the biggest)
               └─────┘          3. read that texel
              −Y (down)

The hardware does all three steps for you: give texture(uSky, dir) any direction and it finds the face that direction stabs through, works out the spot, and returns the texel — with smooth filtering, mipmaps, the works. That's the entire interface. A cubemap is "a function from direction to colour", implemented as six images.

3. Drawing the sky: a cube around the camera

There are two classic ways to paint a cubemap across the background. We use the more direct one for a first cubemap: draw a unit cube centred on the camera. Here's the neat part — we don't need to compute a sample direction at all. A cube corner's object-space position (say (0.5, −0.5, 0.5)) already is a direction pointing out from the cube's centre. So the vertex shader just forwards the raw position; the rasterizer interpolates it across each face into a smooth per-fragment ray, and the fragment shader samples the cubemap with it.

skybox.vert                          skybox.frag
  vDir = inPosition;  ──────────▶       dir = normalize(vDir);
  (cube corner = direction)             outColor = texture(uSky, dir);

(The other technique draws a single fullscreen triangle and reconstructs the ray from the inverse view-projection. It avoids drawing a cube, but needs a matrix inverse our math library doesn't have yet — and "a cube you sample by direction" is the clearer mental model for a first cubemap. We may revisit it later.)

4. Keeping the sky infinitely far: strip the translation

If we drew the cube with the ordinary view matrix, it would be a small box floating at the origin — walk toward it and you'd hit its wall. We want the opposite: a sky that rotates as you turn your head but never slides as you walk. The trick is to feed the skybox the camera's rotation only, with its position removed.

Recall from Step 4 that the view matrix is the camera's inverse: an orientation (its upper-left 3×3) plus a translation (its last column — see Mat4.hpp). Zero that last column and only the orientation survives, so the sky turns with the camera but is pinned at infinity, unreachable:

  Mat4 viewNoTranslation = view;
  viewNoTranslation.at(0,3) = 0;   // wipe the camera's world position
  viewNoTranslation.at(1,3) = 0;   // (the last column of a column-major Mat4)
  viewNoTranslation.at(2,3) = 0;
  skyViewProj = projection * viewNoTranslation;   // pushed to the sky vertex shader

5. Sitting behind everything: the far-plane depth trick

The sky must appear behind all the geometry and hide none of it. Two moving parts make that automatic — and understanding them means recalling the depth buffer (Step 3): each pixel stores the depth of the nearest thing drawn so far, cleared to 1.0 (the far plane) at the start of the frame.

(a) Force the sky's depth to the far plane. After the vertex shader the GPU divides gl_Position by its w (the perspective divide). If we set z = w, that divide yields w / w = 1.0 — the maximum depth — for every sky pixel. A one-token swizzle does it:

  vec4 clip = skyViewProj * vec4(inPosition, 1.0);
  gl_Position = clip.xyww;      // z := w, so post-divide depth = 1.0 (far plane)

(b) Draw the sky last, testing with LEQUAL and writing no depth. We render the scene first, then the sky. By now the depth buffer holds each object's real (nearer-than-1.0) depth, while untouched background pixels are still at the 1.0 clear. The sky, emitting depth 1.0, is kept only where it passes a less-than-or-equal test:

  background pixel:  depth buffer = 1.0,   sky depth 1.0  →  1.0 ≤ 1.0   ✔ drawn
  covered pixel:     depth buffer = 0.37,  sky depth 1.0  →  1.0 ≤ 0.37  ✗ rejected

So the sky lands in exactly the gaps the scene left, and nowhere else. We also turn depth writes off — the sky must not stamp a wall of far-plane depth into the buffer. (The main geometry uses a plain LESS test; the sky needs LESS_OR_EQUAL precisely because its depth equals the clear value it's trying to pass.) Drawing last also means the GPU can skip shading sky pixels that geometry already covered — no wasted work painting behind the torus.

6. No seams: clamp the sampler to the edges

A cubemap's six faces meet along twelve shared edges. If the sampler wrapped texture coordinates past [0,1] (the REPEAT mode our tiled floor uses), a texel at a face's edge would blend with the opposite edge of the same face — producing a visible seam where faces join. The fix is CLAMP_TO_EDGE on all three axes: edge texels clamp to the border, so neighbouring faces line up cleanly. Our main sampler wraps, so the skybox gets its own dedicated clamping sampler.

7. Where the sky comes from — tools/gen_skybox.py

Rather than ship six photographs, a small Python script (run with uv) generates a procedural day sky as six BMP faces — the format our loader reads. The sky is a pure function of direction: a vertical gradient (deep blue overhead fading to a hazy horizon), a muted ground below the horizon, and a sun disk placed at the direction of the scene's actual sun light (so the bright spot in the sky and the shading on the objects agree).

The one subtle part is face orientation. To make six flat images meet seamlessly, the script does exactly what the GPU does — in reverse. For every texel it computes the world-space direction the hardware will later use to sample that texel (using the standard cube-face convention below), then evaluates the sky at that direction. Because generation and sampling share one direction convention, adjacent faces agree along every shared edge with no hand-alignment:

  standard face → direction  (sc across columns, tc down rows):
     +X: ( +1, −tc, −sc)      −X: ( −1, −tc, +sc)
     +Y: (+sc,  +1, +tc)      −Y: (+sc,  −1, −tc)
     +Z: (+sc, −tc,  +1)      −Z: (−sc, −tc,  −1)

A cross-system gotcha: the sky met the bloom threshold

The first attempt made the sky far too pale. The culprit was Step 10's bloom: its bright-pass treats any pixel brighter than a luminance threshold (0.9) as a glowing light source. A realistically pale horizon sailed over that line, so the whole upper sky bloomed and washed to white. The lesson is that new content has to respect the post-processing pipeline it renders into: we deliberately keep the sky's body below the bloom threshold, so it reads as a surface, and reserve the bright range for the one thing meant to glow — the sun disk, which is left near 1.0 so it (and only it) blooms. This is the kind of interaction you only catch by looking at a rendered frame — hence the KOI_CAPTURE check below.

8. Uploading six faces into one cube texture

A cube texture is really six 2D layers. Uploading it is the same staging dance as an ordinary texture (Step 6) — a CPU-visible transfer buffer, a copy pass — with three differences, all in GpuRenderer.cpp (uploadCubemap): the texture's type is CUBE, its layer_count_or_depth is 6, and we run one copy per face into the matching layer. A single SDL_GenerateMipmapsForGPUTexture then fills every layer's mip chain at once (so distant sky stays smooth — and so IBL later has the pre-averaged levels it will need).

The sky is engine-global infrastructure, like the shadow map — so the renderer owns the cube texture, its clamping sampler, and the cube mesh (it reuses makeCubeMesh, reading only the positions). The Engine loads the six face files at scene-build time via loadCubemap(...); if they're missing the engine logs and carries on with the flat clear colour.

9. Try it yourself

  1. Toggle the sky. Run the engine and press 8: the sky vanishes to the flat clear colour and comes back. That's the whole skybox draw, gated on one flag.
  2. Prove it's infinitely far. Fly around with WASD — the sky rotates as you turn but never gets closer. Now comment out the three translation-zeroing lines in recordSkybox (GpuRenderer.cpp), rebuild, and watch the sky turn into a small box you can fly out of.
  3. Recolour the sky. Edit the palette (or the sun direction) in gen_skybox.py, run uv run tools/gen_skybox.py, rebuild, and see a new sky — try pushing a colour past the bloom threshold and watch it glow.
  4. Verify headlessly. KOI_CAPTURE=out.bmp ./build/koi-engine then sips -s format png out.bmp --out out.png — the sky sits behind every object, seamless at the horizon, with the sun's glow blooming near the top.

10. What's next

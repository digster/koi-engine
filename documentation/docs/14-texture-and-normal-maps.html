Texture & Normal Maps

Goal of Step 13: stop describing a surface with a handful of numbers and start describing it per pixel, from images. We add three PBR texture maps — metallic-roughness, ambient occlusion, and the conceptually rich one, a normal map — so a single flat face can vary from polished metal to rough dielectric and show bumps and grooves it doesn't actually have.

Two supporting ideas arrive with them: tangent space and the TBN matrix (what makes a normal map usable), and mipmaps + anisotropic filtering (what stops per-pixel textures from shimmering as they recede).

1. From one number to a whole image

In Step 12 a surface was two dials: one metallic and one roughness value for the entire object. Real surfaces aren't uniform — a scuffed metal panel is glossy on the faces and rough in the grooves; rust is metal in some spots and not in others. To capture that, we let each parameter vary across the surface by reading it from a texture map instead of a constant, sampled at the same uv we already use for the base colour.

We keep the old scalars, but they become factors that multiply the sampled map (glTF calls these metallicFactor / roughnessFactor). So a material can be "the map, scaled by 0.5", or — if it has no map at all — just the scalar, exactly like Step 12. More on that graceful fallback in §7.

The metallic-roughness map (and its packing trick)

Rather than ship two grayscale images, the glTF standard packs both into one texture: the green channel is roughness, the blue channel is metallic (red is left for ambient occlusion or unused). One fetch, two parameters — and because it's the industry convention, the same asset will drop straight into a future glTF importer. Our shader reads metallic = factor.x · tex.b and roughness = factor.y · tex.g.

The ambient-occlusion (AO) map

AO is a grayscale map (we read its red channel) that pre-bakes how exposed each point is to surrounding light: 1.0 in the open, dark in creases and contact points where ambient light can't easily reach. It multiplies only the ambient term — direct light already has the shadow map for occlusion — so it quietly deepens the crevices.

2. Normal maps: detail without geometry

The headline feature. A model's triangles are coarse, but real surfaces have fine relief: brick mortar, fabric weave, pores, panel seams. Adding enough triangles to model all that would be ruinously expensive. A normal map is the classic cheat: an image where each texel stores a surface normal, so lighting is computed as if the surface tilted that way at that pixel — even though the geometry is dead flat. The silhouette stays low-poly, but the shading gains all the bumps.

Recall from Step 7 that lighting is basically N · L: how aligned the surface normal is with the light. If we perturb N per pixel, we perturb the brightness per pixel — and the eye reads that as bumps.

Why "tangent space"?

The obvious idea — store each texel's normal as a world-space direction — fails immediately: the texture would only be correct for one fixed orientation, and couldn't wrap around a curved or moving surface. So normal maps store the normal in tangent space: a coordinate frame that follows the surface, defined relative to how the texture is laid out. In that frame, "straight out of the surface" is always (0, 0, 1) — which is why unmodified normal maps look overwhelmingly blue (the B channel ≈ 1, the flat direction). A texel that tilts the normal a little encodes small deviations in R and G.

3. The TBN matrix

To light with a tangent-space normal we must rotate it into world space, and that rotation is a 3×3 matrix built from three world-space axes at the surface point:

   N  (normal)     — straight out of the surface   (we've had this since Step 7)
   T  (tangent)    — the direction U increases along the surface
   B  (bitangent)  — the direction V increases       (= N × T)

   TBN = [ T  B  N ]   (its columns)

   world_normal = TBN · (texel.rgb · 2 − 1)
                       └── decode [0,1] back to [−1,1] ──┘

Read the multiply column by column: a tangent-space normal (x, y, z) becomes x·T + y·B + z·N. A flat texel (0, 0, 1) maps to just N — the geometric normal, unchanged — while a tilted texel leans toward T or B. The · 2 − 1 undoes the encoding that squeezed the signed direction into an image's [0, 1] byte range.

N we already output. B is just N × T. The one genuinely new thing we need is T, the tangent — and unlike the normal, it can't be guessed from the geometry alone, because it's defined by which way the UVs run across the surface. That's why Step 13 touches so much: the tangent has to become a per-vertex attribute.

A simplification we make: we store the tangent as a plain vec3 and reconstruct the bitangent as N × T. That assumes the UVs have a consistent handedness; a surface with mirrored UVs would need a signed (vec4) tangent whose sign flips the bitangent. It's the right call for our meshes — noted so you know the corner we cut.

Where the tangent comes from

  • Our primitives (cube, plane) are axis-aligned with trivial UVs, so we read the tangent off by inspection — the model-space direction of increasing U on each face — and hardcode it beside the normal, in Primitives.cpp.
  • Loaded models compute it from the geometry: for each triangle, solve for the direction that maps the texture's U-axis onto the surface using the edge vectors and the matching UV deltas (the standard "Lengyel" method), accumulate it per shared vertex, then re-orthonormalise it against the normal. glTF files that already ship a TANGENT attribute are used directly. The pure math lives in Tangents.hpp, unit-tested headlessly like our other shader twins.

4. Mipmaps: why minified textures shimmer

Now that per-pixel detail dominates the image, a sampling problem that was always lurking gets loud. When a textured surface recedes into the distance, one screen pixel can cover many texels. Sampling just one of them is a coin-flip — and as the surface (or camera) moves, which texel "wins" flickers, so distant textures crawl and sparkle. This is aliasing: we're point-sampling a signal far denser than our pixels.

The fix is a mipmap chain: the texture pre-shrunk to ½, ¼, ⅛ … down to 1×1, each level a properly averaged version of the one above.

   level 0        level 1     level 2   level 3  …
   ┌────────┐     ┌────┐      ┌──┐      ┌┐
   │ 256²   │  →  │128²│  →   │64│  →   ││  →  … → 1×1
   └────────┘     └────┘      └──┘      └┘
   full detail    each level = the previous one, box-averaged down

When a surface is far away the GPU reads a smaller, pre-averaged level, so one pixel gets the average of the texels it covers instead of a random one — steady, not sparkly. With trilinear filtering the sampler even blends between the two nearest levels so the transition is smooth. We generate the whole chain at upload time with one call (SDL_GenerateMipmapsForGPUTexture); the texture just needs to be created with enough levels (and, so the GPU can render the smaller ones, the extra "color target" usage flag).

5. Anisotropic filtering: the grazing-angle blur

Mipmaps fix shimmer but introduce their own artifact. A mip level is shrunk equally in both directions — it's square. But a floor viewed at a shallow angle is compressed a lot along the direction going away from you and barely at all across your view. A plain mipmapped sampler has to pick one level for the whole pixel, so to avoid shimmer along the squashed axis it over-blurs the other — and the receding floor turns to mush.

Anisotropic ("not the same in all directions") filtering fixes this by taking several taps along the stretched direction and averaging them, keeping the sharp axis sharp. It's a quality knob — max_anisotropy caps how many taps (we use 8). Enabling it on our one shared sampler is what keeps the tiled floor crisp all the way to the horizon.

6. Putting it in the shader

  // build the TBN and perturb the geometric normal
  N = normalize(vWorldNormal);
  T = normalize(vWorldTangent − N · dot(N, vWorldTangent));   // Gram-Schmidt
  B = cross(N, T);
  N = normalize( mat3(T,B,N) · (texture(uNormalMap, uv).xyz · 2 − 1) );

  // per-pixel material (glTF packing), scaled by the scalar factors
  mr        = texture(uMetalRoughMap, uv).rgb;
  metallic  = factor.x · mr.b;
  roughness = factor.y · mr.g;
  ao        = texture(uAoMap, uv).r;

  // …then the exact same Cook-Torrance loop as Step 12, using this N…
  color = ambient · albedo · ao + Lo;      // AO darkens only the ambient fill

The Cook-Torrance BRDF itself is unchanged — Step 13 only changes where the inputs come from (a perturbed N, per-pixel metallic/ roughness). That's the payoff of building on a physically-based model: richer inputs slot straight in.

7. The fallback trick: optional maps, no shader branches

Not every material has every map. The tempting fix — a "has normal map?" boolean uniform and an if in the shader — is a trap: each such flag doubles the number of shader variants the engine must manage. We avoid it entirely with neutral fallback textures. The renderer owns two 1×1 textures: white (255,255,255) and a flat normal (128,128,255). A material that omits a map binds the fallback, and the maths above reduces exactly to the scalar-only Step 12 result:

  • flat normal decodes to (0,0,1) → N stays the geometric normal;
  • white metallic-roughness → factor · 1 = the scalar factor;
  • white AO → 1, no darkening.

One code path, correct for materials with maps and without. In the demo the dotted satellite cubes, sphere, and torus take this path and look identical to Step 12; only the hub cube and floor carry the full "tiled panel" map set.

8. The code

The tangent — Vertex.hpp + Tangents.hpp

Vertex grew a fifth attribute, tangent (pitch 44 → 56 bytes), added last so earlier offsets hold; the pipeline's vertex layout and test_vertex.cpp pin the new contract. Tangents.hpp holds the pure tangent math (unit-tested in test_tangent.cpp); primitives hardcode their tangents, ModelLoader computes or reads them.

The material — scene/Material.hpp

Alongside the albedo it now carries three optional map handles (metalRough, normalMap, ao); the metallic/ roughness scalars became factors. The maps go last so existing positional initialisers keep working.

The renderer — GpuRenderer.cpp

The fragment shader now reads five samplers (four per-material maps at slots 0–3, the shadow map moved to slot 4). uploadToGpuTexture gained mip generation, the shared sampler gained anisotropy, and the renderer creates the two neutral fallback textures. recordNode binds each material's four maps per draw, substituting a fallback for any that are null.

The maps — tools/gen_textures.py

A small Python script (run with uv) generates the demo's "tiled panel" maps — a beveled normal map, a packed metallic-roughness map whose tiles alternate metal/dielectric, an AO map, and a plain albedo — as BMPs (the format our loader reads).

9. Try it yourself

  1. Kill the bump. Point the hub's normalMap at the flat fallback (or set it null) in Engine.cpp and rebuild: the beveled tile relief flattens to a plain cube face — that's the normal map's whole contribution.
  2. Deepen the relief. Raise BUMP_STRENGTH in gen_textures.py, regenerate, and watch the grooves get steeper.
  3. See the shimmer mipmaps prevent. Force num_levels = 1 (no mip chain) and turn enable_anisotropy off, then fly toward the horizon: the tiled floor starts to crawl and sparkle — the aliasing §4 and §5 exist to fix.
  4. Verify headlessly. KOI_CAPTURE=out.bmp ./build/koi-engine then sips -s format png out.bmp --out out.png — the hub cube shows the metal/dielectric checker and beveled tiles side by side with the map-less satellites.
